# Evidence Email Examples

Reference examples of real psychosocial consultation evidence emails.
These inform the tone, structure, and content expectations for the `/draft/email` prompt.

---

## Example 1 — Investigations function manager

Dear Trent,

Thank you for meeting with me on 14 January as part of the Work Health and Safety Management System (WHSMS) project, and for sharing your experiences from within the investigations function.

I appreciated the candid discussion and your insights as a manager supporting a small team working with complex and often contentious matters. Key points from our conversation included:

• Nature of investigative work and psychosocial risk
– Exposure to contentious, distressing, and emotionally charged material as a core part of investigation work
– The cumulative psychosocial impact of sustained exposure over time, particularly where matters are complex, adversarial, or protracted
– The importance of recognising this exposure as a foreseeable psychosocial hazard rather than an individual resilience issue

• Work practices and physical risk
– Concerns regarding work from home arrangements and how these intersect with supervision, support, and visibility of emerging risk
– Musculoskeletal injury risks associated with prolonged screen-based work, both in the office and when working remotely
– The interaction between physical discomfort, sustained cognitive load, and psychosocial strain

• Manager responsibilities and escalation
– Discussion of WHS duties as they apply to managers in identifying hazards, monitoring team wellbeing, and escalating risk
– Gaps in clear escalation and de-escalation processes for complex or high-impact investigative matters
– The need for clearer guidance on when matters require additional support, review, or redistribution

• Capability and system support
– Opportunities to strengthen WHS and psychosocial capability for managers and teams working in investigative roles
– The value of practical tools, guidance, and consistent processes to support decision-making under pressure

• Project status and forward outlook
– An overview of the current stage of the WHSMS project
– The planned focus over the next six months, including further consultation, system development, and staged implementation
– Early indication that additional consultation activities will occur, including focus groups and surveys, to support a more complete psychosocial risk management approach

Thank you again for your time and considered input. Your perspective is important in ensuring the WHSMS appropriately addresses the psychosocial and physical risks associated with investigative work and provides managers with clear, practical support.

If there is anything from our discussion that I have missed, or if there are additional points you would like to add or clarify, please let me know and I would be happy to follow up.

Kind regards,
Niru

## Example 2 — HSW Committee member

Dear Katherine

Thank you for meeting with me on 14 January as part of the Work Health and Safety Management System (WHSMS) project, and for sharing your insights informed by both your role and your experience as a member of the HSW Committee.

I appreciated the practical and well-considered nature of our discussion. Key points from our conversation included:

• WHS awareness and committee perspective
– Your familiarity with existing WHS arrangements and consultation processes through the HSW Committee
– Observations on how psychosocial risks are currently understood and managed in practice
– The value of clear, accessible guidance for staff rather than overly complex or restrictive mechanisms

• Psychosocial risk and customer interactions
– Recognition that exposure to contentious or challenging customers is a foreseeable part of OIC's work
– Your view that a clear, practical fact sheet outlining escalation pathways for contentious customer interactions would be more effective than a caution list
– The importance of clarity so staff understand when and how to escalate matters without feeling they must tolerate unreasonable behaviour at the expense of their wellbeing

• Injury management and return to work
– Discussion of return-to-work processes and the importance of having clear, consistent procedures in place
– Identification of gaps where return-to-work arrangements may not be sufficiently defined or applied, highlighting an opportunity for system improvement

• Capability and system improvement
– The need to continue strengthening WHS and psychosocial capability across the organisation
– Ensuring systems support early identification of risk, clear escalation, and practical guidance for both workers and managers

• Project status and forward outlook
– An overview of the current stage of the WHSMS project
– The planned focus over the coming months, including further consultation, system development, and staged implementation

Thank you again for your time and constructive input. Your perspective will assist in shaping WHS arrangements that are practical, proportionate, and aligned with how work is actually done across OIC.

If there is anything from our discussion that I have missed, or if there are additional points you would like to add or clarify, please let me know and I would be happy to follow up.

Kind regards,
Niru

## Example 3 — Legal and governance

Dear Cecelia,

Thank you for meeting with me on 14 January as part of the Work Health and Safety Management System (WHSMS) project, and for sharing your perspectives from a legal and governance standpoint.

I appreciated your insights into the importance of a robust legal foundation for the WHSMS, particularly given the nature of OIC's work and the circumstances where Crown Law has previously been engaged in relation to WHS matters. Key points from our discussion included:

• Legal oversight and governance risk
– Recognition that WHS risks, including psychosocial risk, can escalate to matters requiring external legal involvement if not managed systematically
– The importance of clear governance settings to reduce legal exposure and avoid reactive responses

• WHS risk appetite and legal defensibility
– Discussion on the need for a clearly articulated WHS risk appetite to guide decision-making and escalation
– The value of ensuring risk tolerance is explicit, defensible, and aligned with statutory obligations rather than implied through practice

• Ad hoc approaches as a risk
– Your observation that WHS activities occurring in an ad hoc or informal manner present a risk in themselves
– The legal and organisational vulnerability created when decisions, controls, and responses are not consistently documented or applied

• Legal underpinning of the WHSMS
– Your willingness to support the development of a legally robust WHSMS, including alignment with WHS legislation and codes of practice
– The opportunity to embed legal clarity into system design to support consistent application and executive assurance

• Project status and forward outlook
– An overview of the current stage of the WHSMS project
– The anticipated focus over the coming months, including further consultation, system development, and staged implementation

Thank you again for your time and considered input. Your perspective will be important in ensuring the WHSMS is legally sound, defensible, and capable of reducing both operational and legal risk through consistent, system-based approaches.

If there is anything from our discussion that I have missed, or if there are additional considerations you believe should be incorporated, please let me know and I would be happy to follow up.

Kind regards,
Niru

## Example 4 — Privacy function leader

Dear Jane,

Please accept my apologies for the delay in sending this note. I was on leave and returned last week.

Thank you for meeting with me on 16 January as part of the Work Health and Safety Management System (WHSMS) project, and for sharing your insights from the Privacy function.

I appreciated the candid discussion and your perspective as Acting Assistant Commissioner leading a team of four. Key points from our conversation included:

• Workforce structure and workload pressures
– The workforce restructure approximately 12 months ago and the ongoing impact of roles remaining unfilled
– Sustained higher workloads contributing to fatigue across the team
– The risk that prolonged capacity strain becomes normalised

• Nature of privacy work and psychosocial exposure
– Exposure to sensitive and often complex subject matter as an inherent part of privacy regulation and complaint handling
– Recognition that while this exposure is foreseeable within the role, it can carry an emotional toll over time
– The importance of acknowledging inherent job risk without framing it as an individual resilience issue

• Change and system maturity
– Possible indicators of change fatigue within the team
– Policies and procedures that may require review and update to ensure clarity, consistency, and alignment with current practice
– The interaction between workload, process gaps, and psychosocial risk

• Escalation and capability
– The need for clear escalation pathways where workload or matter sensitivity exceeds sustainable levels
– Opportunity to strengthen WHS and psychosocial capability for leaders and staff within the privacy function

• Project status and forward outlook
– An overview of the current stage of the WHSMS project
– The planned focus over the coming months, including further consultation, system refinement, and staged implementation

Thank you again for your time and considered input. Your perspective is important in ensuring the WHSMS reflects the realities of privacy work, addresses sustained workload exposure, and supports practical, sustainable risk management.

If there is anything from our discussion that I have missed, or if there are additional points you would like to add, please let me know and I would be happy to follow up.

Kind regards,
Niru

## Patterns observed

- **Structure:** Greeting → thank you + date + project context → acknowledgment of their role → themed bullet points with sub-bullets → closing thank you → invitation to correct/add → sign-off
- **Tone:** Formal but warm. Clinical enough for evidence, human enough to feel respectful.
- **Themes:** Presented as bold headings with dash-prefixed sub-points elaborating specifics
- **Evidence quality:** Makes clear *what* was discussed, not just *that* something was discussed
- **No filler:** No "I hope this finds you well" — gets straight to substance
- **Closing:** Always invites correction or addition — positions the email as a collaborative record
- **First person:** Written from the consultant's perspective ("Thank you for meeting with me")
