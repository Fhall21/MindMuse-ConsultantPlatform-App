  ### **Just Culture Approach**

* **Best for:** A fair and accountable response framework for when things go wrong (e.g. an incident, error or misconduct), preventing a culture of fear that leads to hidden risks.  
* **Steps:**  
  * **System Inquiry:** Investigating the context and pressures rather than blaming the individual immediately.  
  * **Human error (unintentional):** Consoling and supporting; treating it as a system learning opportunity.  
  * **At-risk behaviour (shortcuts/workarounds):** Coaching; understanding the motivation (usually efficiency) and correcting the risk perception.  
  * **Reckless behaviour (willful misconduct):** Disciplining; holding account for intentional disregard of safety.  
* **Sentence Starters:**  
  * *System Inquiry:* "Help me understand the context around this event; what pressures were you under?" / "What made sense to you at the time?"  
  * *Human error:* "We need to understand how the system let this happen to you." / "Let's figure out the fix, not the fault."  
  * *At-risk behaviour:* "I noticed you skipped the safety check; talk me through why that happened." / "Did you feel you didn't have time to do it the long way?"  
  * *Reckless behaviour:* "This behaviour clearly violates our core safety values and cannot be accepted." / "We have a zero tolerance for putting others in danger intentionally."  
* **Sentence Follow-ups:**  
  * *System Inquiry:* "Were you fatigued or distracted by other tasks?"  
  * *Human error:* "What simple check could we add so this doesn't happen to anyone else?"  
  * *At-risk behaviour:* "I understand the pressure, but we can't trade safety for speed; let's fix the timeline instead."  
  * *Reckless behaviour:* (Usually followed by formal HR process steps).  
* **Avoid:**  
  * **Falling into a blame culture reflex.** Immediately punishing errors drives risks underground.  
    * *Example:* Avoid "You should have known better"; try "What information did you need that you didn't have?"  
  * **Patronising language.** Be careful with "honest slip-up" if the person feels they were set up to fail.  
    * *Example:* Avoid assuming it was a "silly mistake"; ask "How easy was it to make that error given the tool design?"
